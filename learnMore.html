Explore Sun Structure

The internal structure of the Sun: Core, Radiation Zone, Convection Zone, Chromosphere, Photosphere, and Corona.

Core
The innermost region of the Sun. Temperature: ~15 million Â°C. This is where nuclear fusion happens: hydrogen atoms fuse to form helium, releasing enormous amounts of energy in the form of light and heat. It's the true powerhouse of the Sun.
Radiation Zone
The layer surrounding the core. Energy produced in the core moves outward in the form of radiation (photons). Photons can take thousands to millions of years to pass through this zone because they constantly collide with particles.
Convection Zone
Above the radiation zone. Energy is transported by convection currents: hot plasma rises, cools as it nears the surface, and sinks again. This movement gives the Sun its granulated surface appearance.
Photosphere
The visible surface of the Sun (what we see with our eyes). Temperature: ~5,500 Â°C. Sunspots (cooler, darker regions) appear here. It emits the sunlight that reaches Earth.
Chromosphere
A thin layer above the photosphere. Temperature: ~6,000-20,000 Â°C. Appears as a reddish glow during a solar eclipse. Source of solar prominences and flares.
Corona
The Sun's outermost atmosphere, extending millions of kilometers into space. Temperature: over 1 million Â°C (hotter than the surface due to magnetic activity). Visible during a total solar eclipse as a halo. Source of the solar wind that flows throughout the solar system.
